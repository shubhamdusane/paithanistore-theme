The Nauvari saree — nine yards of pure Maharashtrian tradition — can feel intimidating to drape for the first time. But once you understand the steps, it becomes second nature. This guide walks you through everything.

## What is a Nauvari Saree?

A Nauvari (नऊवारी) saree is a 9-yard long saree traditionally worn in Maharashtra. Unlike the 6-yard saree most people know, the extra length enables the iconic Kashtha draping style — where the fabric is tucked between the legs, creating a trouser-like silhouette that allows easy movement.

The word Nauvari comes from "Nau" (nine) and "Vari" (yards). It is also called 9-vari saree, Navvari saree, Kashtha saree, or Lugade.

---

## What You Need Before You Start

- Your 9-yard Nauvari saree
- A well-fitted petticoat tied firmly at the waist
- Safety pins (5 to 7 for beginners)
- A fitted blouse
- Low to medium heels

---

## Step-by-Step: Traditional Kashtha Draping

**Step 1: Start at the back**
Hold the saree at one short end. Tuck this end firmly into the back center of your petticoat, inserting at least 3–4 inches. This is your anchor.

**Step 2: Wrap to the front**
Bring the saree around your right side to the front. Make a full circle around your waist, keeping the fabric even and parallel to the floor.

**Step 3: Create front pleats**
Fold 5 to 7 neat pleats in the saree (each pleat about 5 inches wide). Tuck them into the front center of the petticoat, pointing slightly to the left.

**Step 4: The leg tuck (what makes it a Nauvari)**
Take the remaining fabric and bring it between your legs from front to back. Tuck firmly into the back of your petticoat. Use a safety pin at the back for security.

**Step 5: The upper drape**
Bring the remaining fabric (the pallav end) up from behind and over your left shoulder. The pallav should hang down your back.

**Step 6: Secure and adjust**
Pin the pallav at the shoulder. Adjust front pleats so they fall neatly.

## Styling Guide

**For Weddings (Bridal):** Pure silk Paithani Nauvari in red, maroon, or green. Heavy embroidered blouse. Traditional gold jewellery — thushi, nath, bangles.

**For Festivals (Ganesh Chaturthi, Gudi Padwa):** Semi-silk or cotton Nauvari in bright yellow, orange, or pink. Simple blouse, minimal jewellery.

**For Daily / Cultural Events:** Cotton Nauvari in subtle checks or prints.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Petticoat too loose — always tie firmly first
2. Not enough fabric in the leg tuck — causes drape to open when walking
3. Pallav too short or too long — ideally 12 to 16 inches from shoulder
4. Skipping safety pins — beginners should always use them
5. Wrong fabric for first attempt — start with cotton, not silk
